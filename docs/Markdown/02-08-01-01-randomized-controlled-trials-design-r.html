    1.1 Design Phase of Randomized Controlled Trials (RCTs)

    Designing a rigorous Randomized Controlled Trial (RCT) is a critical first step in ensuring valid and reliable causal inference. This tutorial provides a comprehensive guide to the design phase of RCTs using R, covering hypothesis formulation, population definition, intervention specification, outcome selection, and sample size calculation through power analysis.

    Overview

    The design phase is the foundation of any rigorous Randomized Controlled Trial (RCT). Before enrolling a single participant, researchers must clearly define:

    • The scientific hypothesis
    • The target population
    • The intervention and control condition
    • The primary outcome
    • The randomization strategy
    • The required sample size (via power analysis)

    Goal: By the end of this tutorial, you will be able to specify your RCT design in R and prepare a reproducible protocol template.

    Theoretical Foundations

    Why the Design Phase Matters

    Randomization only guarantees unbiased causal estimates if the trial is properly designed. Poorly defined outcomes, vague populations, or underpowered samples can lead to:

    • False negatives (Type II errors)
    • Inability to generalize results
    • Ethical concerns (exposing participants to risk without adequate scientific justification)

    Key Design Elements (Per CONSORT & ICH-GCP Guidelines)

    Element Purpose
    Hypothesis Specifies the causal question (e.g., “Drug X reduces systolic BP vs. placebo”)
    Target Population Defines eligibility criteria (inclusion/exclusion)
    Intervention & Control Describes what is delivered (dose, frequency, duration)
    Primary Outcome The main endpoint used to test the hypothesis (must be pre-specified!)
    Sample Size Determined by desired power, significance level, effect size, and variability

    The design phase of a randomized controlled trial (RCT) and an observational study

    The design phase of a randomized controlled trial (RCT) and an observational study differs fundamentally in purpose, feasibility, assumptions, and methodology—even when addressing the same scientific question (e.g., Does X affect lung cancer mortality?).

    Below is a detailed comparison focused on two scenarios:

    • RCT: Testing the efficacy of a new drug on lung cancer mortality
    • Observational study: Assessing the effect of smoking on lung cancer incidence/mortality

    Core Philosophical Difference

    Aspect RCT (Drug Trial) Observational Study (Smoking)
    Goal Estimate efficacy under ideal conditions Estimate effectiveness or causal association in real-world settings
    Assignment Researcher controls treatment assignment (randomization) Treatment (smoking) is self-selected; no intervention
    Feasibility of Randomization Ethically and practically possible (with informed consent) Unethical and impossible to randomize people to smoke for decades

    Key Insight: You can randomize a drug, but you cannot (and should not) randomize smoking behavior over a lifetime.

    Design Phase Components Compared

    Hypothesis Formulation

    RCT Observational
    “Drug X reduces 2-year lung cancer mortality compared to placebo in stage III NSCLC patients.”
    → Precise, testable, time-bound     		“Long-term cigarette smoking increases the risk of lung cancer mortality in adults.”
    → Broader, often based on existing associations

    Target Population

    RCT Observational
    Highly selected: Strict inclusion/exclusion criteria (e.g., age 40–75, confirmed NSCLC, ECOG performance status ≤2, no prior chemo) Broad & heterogeneous: General population or cohort (e.g., all adult smokers in a region), often with minimal exclusion

    RCTs prioritize internal validity; observational studies prioritize external validity/generalizability.

    Intervention / Exposure Definition

    RCT Observational
    Standardized: Fixed dose, route, duration, monitoring (e.g., “500 mg oral Drug X daily for 6 months”) Variable & self-reported: Smoking defined by pack-years, current/former/never status—subject to recall bias and misclassification

    Control / Comparison Group

    RCT Observational
    Active control: Placebo or standard-of-care, blinded if possible Non-exposed group: Never-smokers—but may differ in health behaviors (e.g., diet, exercise), creating confounding

    Outcome Definition

    Both typically use similar endpoints (e.g., overall survival, lung cancer-specific mortality), but:
    RCT: Outcomes assessed prospectively, using protocol-defined criteria, centralized review

    Confounding Control Strategy

    RCT Observational
    Prevention via randomization: Balances known & unknown confounders on average Adjustment after data collection: Relies on measuring and modeling confounders (e.g., age, asbestos exposure, alcohol)—residual confounding likely

    In smoking studies, unmeasured confounders (e.g., genetic predisposition, occupational hazards) are a major concern.

    Sample Size & Power Calculation

    RCT Observational
    Mandatory: Based on expected effect size, α, power, variability Often absent or post-hoc: Cohort size may be fixed (e.g., existing database); power rarely calculated prospectively

    Ethical & Regulatory Requirements

    RCT Observational
    Requires IRB/ethics approval, informed consent, DSMB, pre-registration (e.g., ClinicalTrials.gov) May be exempt from full IRB review if using de-identified data; consent often waived; pre-registration less common

    Why You Can’t Use an RCT for Smoking effect on LBC mortlity rtes

    • Ethics: Deliberately assigning people to smoke would cause known harm—violates the principle of non-maleficence.
    • Duration: Lung cancer develops over decades—RCTs this long are impractical.
    • Compliance: Participants randomized to “smoke” may quit; those in “non-smoke” may start—diluting effect.
    • Historical evidence: The causal link was established through convergent evidence (cohort studies, biological plausibility, dose-response, consistency)—not RCTs.

    This is why Bradford Hill criteria were developed—to infer causality without RCTs.

    Summary Table: Design Phase Differences

    Feature RCT (Drug for Lung Cancer) Observational (Smoking & Lung Cancer)
    Assignment Randomized by investigator Self-selected (exposure)
    Control of Confounding By design (randomization) By analysis (adjustment)
    Population Narrow, homogeneous Broad, heterogeneous
    Intervention Standardized, monitored Variable, self-reported
    Blinding Often possible Not possible
    Bias Risk Low (selection, confounding) High (confounding, selection, information bias)
    Causal Strength High (if well-conducted) Moderate (requires strong assumptions)
    Feasibility Possible with resources Only ethical/practical option for exposures like smoking

    Define the Hypothesis

    Formulate a testable, directional hypothesis:

    Participants randomized to the mindfulness app will show a greater reduction in GAD-7 score at 12 weeks compared to those in the waitlist control group.

    In statistical terms:

    \[ H_0: \mu_{\text{treatment}} - \mu_{\text{control}} = 0 \quad \text{vs.} \quad H_A: \mu_{\text{treatment}} - \mu_{\text{control}} < 0 \]

    We expect a negative difference because lower GAD-7 = less anxiety)

    • *Best Practice**: Pre-register your hypothesis on ClinicalTrials.gov or OSF.

    Define the Target Population

    Specify inclusion/exclusion criteria. For our example:

    Code
    # Example eligibility criteria (for documentation, not execution)
inclusion_criteria <- c(
  "Age 18–65",
  "GAD-7 score between 5 and 14 (mild-to-moderate anxiety)",
  "Owns smartphone with iOS/Android",
  "Provides informed consent"
)

exclusion_criteria <- c(
  "Current psychotherapy or psychiatric medication",
  "History of severe mental illness (e.g., psychosis, bipolar disorder)",
  "GAD-7 ≥ 15 (severe anxiety)"
)

# Store in a list for protocol documentation
trial_population <- list(
  inclusion = inclusion_criteria,
  exclusion = exclusion_criteria,
  target_n = NULL  # To be filled after power analysis
)

    This list becomes part of your study protocol and informs recruitment scripts.

    Define Intervention and Control

    Be specific about what, how, when, and by whom:

    Code
    intervention <- list(
  name = "Mindfulness App (‘CalmMind’)",
  description = "Daily 10-min guided meditation + weekly psychoeducation module",
  duration_weeks = 12,
  delivery = "Mobile application (iOS/Android)",
  adherence_tracking = "App usage logs (sessions completed)"
)

control <- list(
  name = "Waitlist Control",
  description = "No active intervention; offered app after 12 weeks",
  duration_weeks = 12,
  delivery = "Standard care",
  adherence_tracking = "None"
)

conditions <- list(intervention = intervention, control = control)

    Note: The control condition must be ethically justifiable and clearly defined.

    Define the Primary Outcome

    Choose one primary outcome to avoid multiplicity issues. It should be:

    • Clinically meaningful
    • Reliable and valid
    • Measured consistently

    For our trial:

    Code
    primary_outcome <- list(
  name = "Change in GAD-7 score from baseline to week 12",
  type = "continuous",
  scale = "0–21 (higher = more anxiety)",
  measurement_timepoint = "12 weeks post-randomization",
  analysis_metric = "mean difference (treatment - control)"
)

    Critical: This outcome must be pre-specified before data collection begins.

    Power Analysis & Sample Size Calculation

    We now estimate the required sample size using the pwr package.

    Assumptions (based on pilot data or literature): - Expected mean GAD-7 reduction: 3.0 points in treatment vs. 1.0 in control → effect size = 2.0 - Standard deviation of change scores: 4.0 - Two-sided α = 0.05, Power = 80% (β = 0.20)

    First, compute Cohen’s d which define as a standardized measure of effect size that quantifies the difference between two group means in terms of standard deviation units. It is widely used in psychology, medicine, education, and social sciences to assess the magnitude of an intervention or association—especially in t-tests, power analyses, and meta-analyses.

    \[ d = \frac{\text{Mean Difference}}{\text{SD}} = \frac{2.0}{4.0} = 0.5 \quad \text{(medium effect)} \]

    Now calculate sample size per group:

    Code
    # Install if needed
# install.packages("pwr")
library(pwr)

# Two-sample t-test (parallel design)
power_result <- pwr.t.test(
  d = 0.5,           # Effect size (Cohen's d)
  sig.level = 0.05,  # Alpha
  power = 0.80,      # Desired power
  type = "two.sample",
  alternative = "two.sided"
)

# View result
print(power_result)

    Document Your Design in R Markdown

    Create a reproducible protocol draft:

    Code
    # Save key design elements
design_summary <- list(
  hypothesis = "Mindfulness app reduces GAD-7 vs. waitlist at 12 weeks",
  population = trial_population,
  conditions = conditions,
  outcome = primary_outcome,
  sample_size = total_n_needed,
  alpha = 0.05,
  power = 0.80,
  effect_size_d = 0.5
)

# Optional: Write to YAML for sharing
yaml::write_yaml(design_summary, "rct_protocol_design.yaml")

    Summary and Conclusion

    In the design phase of an RCT, clarity and precision are paramount. Using R, you can:

    • Formally define your hypothesis, population, intervention, and outcome
    • Compute scientifically justified sample sizes
    • Store design parameters in structured, reproducible objects
    • Generate protocol documents programmatically

    This upfront rigor ensures your trial is ethical, efficient, and interpretable—and sets the stage for robust randomization and analysis in later phases.
